The ectoproct (bryozoan) taxonomic composition of the two phases also differs, as shown in table 2. The relative importance of the bryozoa in each phase is indicated in table 2 by mean-rank-abundance {see Bambach, Calef, and %'alker, 1972). This index is calculated by sum- ming the rank abundance of a given genus in each sample (most abun- dant genus = I, second most abundant = 2, et cetera) and clividing the total for each genus by the number of samples. Mean-rank-abundance gives an index of the average dominance of the genus in all samples. The most abundant genera are illustrated in plate S.

The bryozoaon taxonomic composition of the Alcoa Highway core is quite variable from collection to collection. Table 2A indicates the average composition and shows that ramose Stictopora has the highest mean-rank abundance, followed by ramose Bythopora and the incrust- ing genus Amptexopora (pl. 5-0). The remaining occasional genera in- crease the total for the core to eighteen, For this suite of 6 samples, how- ever, mean-rank-abundances are somewhat misleading. Four of the six samples have an incrusting bryozoan as the most abundant genus (Am- pexopora in three samples and Monotrypa (pl. 3-G) in one); but be- cause Stictopora is uniformly the second or third most abundant, its mean-rank-abundance is high. It is also notable that, of the genera that occur in both Hank and core (14), Hemiphragma (pl. S-F), Amp/exopora, and Batostorna (pl. 5-D) occur as incrusters in the core and as ramose forms in the Hank. In addition, Stictopora (pl. S-B) takes an incrusting growth form only in the reef core.

In the Hank collections, the bryozoan fauna is much more constant in composition. Stictopora (pl. 3-8 and -M), a bifoliate, ramose, cryp- tostome is uniformly the most abundant bryozoan in the flank deposits followed closely by Bythopora {pl. S-O), a ramose trepostome. Ramose forms of the trepostome Hemiphragma and a peculiar semi-bifoliate, cryptostome, Ottoseetaxis {pl. 5-K), are the third and fourth most abun- dant bryozoans. The rarer bryozoans present extend the number of gen- era in flank deposits to eighteen (table 2). The incrusting genera (for example Mesotrypa, pl. 9-E) that occur in the flank deposits are frag- mented, abraded, randomly oriented, and obviously not in situ.
