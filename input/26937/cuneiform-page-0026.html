The scale of the Holston reef mass indicates, as previously noted, a reef tract of impressive size and duration.

The taxonomic composition of the New York Chazyan reefs also differs markedly from that of the Holston. The Chazy contains a rela- tively diverse fauna consisting of incrusting Bryozoa (Batostoma and Cheiloporella), stromatoporoids (Cystostroma and Pseudostylodictyon), corals (Lichenaria, IIillingsaria, and EoPetcharia), sponges (Zettelella), and red algae (Solenogora). In contrast, the Holston reef faunas are less diverse in terms of higher taxonomic categories (for example, phyla or classes), since they are overwhehningly dominated by Bryozoa; yet at the generic level (see tables 2 and 3), they are much more diverse than the Chazyan faunas. This great dominance of a single adaptive type of organism may reflect a more stringent physical environment for the Holston or could indicate the establishment of the shelf-edge reef en- vironment only shortly before or concurrent with the Holston deposi- tion. In the latter case, the shelf-edge fauna would not have had suffi- cient time to diversify. The limited data now available make the sec- ond possibility more likely, for there is little evidence that the Holston physical environment was more severe than that of the Chazy. A large shelf-interior reef in the Carters Formation of south-central Tennessee (approximately correlative with the Holston), which is presently being studied in detail by L, P. Alberstadt and co-workers, shows diversity at higher taxonomic levels similar to that of the New York Chazy. Thus, the high generic diversity but low phylum-class diversity of the Holston reefs cannot be related to some peculiarity of the entire reef biota in the Middle Ordovcian.

SUMMARY

The Holston Formation of east Tennessee has long been the sub- ject of stratigraphic controversy concerning its classification and origin. We have adopted the classification of Cattermole {1958) in the study area who separated the lower, purer, carbonate Holston Formation from the overlying, quartzose carbonate Chapman Ridge Formation. Although several previous authors have speculated on the reefal origin of the Holston, no previous detailed environmental analysis has been done. Standard field-stratigraphic teclmiques are difficult to apply to the For- mation because of rapid vertical and lateral facies changes and highly variable cross-bed clips in a single outcrop. We have, therefore, studied four representative localities in the northeast-southwest strike belt south of Knoxville in some detail and based most of our treatment on two of these.

Presence of Amplexopora conferta, A. superba, Batostoma lanieusis, Monotrypa undulatus, and Mesotiypa regularis, as well as distinctive species of Stictojora suggest correlation of the Holston with the upper Wilderness Stage (middle Champlainian Series = middle Caradocian Series of the Welsh type Ordovician).

The formation is composed of two basic lithologies representing two reef-related subenvironments. One is a pink to maroon (rarely white), ectoproct Bryozoa boundstone composed of SO to 40 percent lime-mudstone bound together by 30 to 40 percent incrusting bryozoan colonies. These boundstone masses or cores are rarely separate dome- shaped structures but usually form a three-dimensional network which anastomoses upward through the formation with individual "arms" of the net up to 3 m in width. Surrounding the boundstone cores are cross beds of pink to white pelmatozoan jbryozoan calcarenite which dip away from the closest core at angles of 25' to 45'. These cross beds indicate that some of the cores stood as much as 6 to 7 m above adjacent sea-bottom. The entire complex of anastomosing cores and associated flank deposits compose a stratigraphic unit 100 m thick with an outcrop exposure at least 40 km long. The reef cores possessed the ecological potential of being wave resistant and, therefore, were biohermal reefs {term after Nelson, Brown, and Brineman, 1962). Thus, the mass repre- sents a major reef tract. Associated stratigraphic units indicate that this reef tract formed at the eastern edge of a carbonate bank and was bounded on the east by a basin where fine-grained terrigenous clastics derived from the southeast were being trapped in slightly deeper water. As time passed, the basin filled, and terrigenous clastics began to im- pinge on the bank edge and cover the reefs. The reefs retreated onto the former bank to the west but were finally smothered and disappeared from the facies pattern.
