In analyzing the depositional environment of the Holston, two localities have been investigated in detail. These sections were selected on the basis of their accessibility and excellence of exposure. Other localities are mentioned in the text only when they show unique fea- tures not present at our two primary localities. The first is locality 4 of table I, which is a small individual reef mound in which the various parts of the complex are easily visualized. At this locality, referred to hereafter as the Alcoa I-Iighway reef, the small core and associated flank beds are surrounded by larger, more complex bodies. The second out- crop is our locality 3 of table 1. This locality, hereafter called the Deane Quarry reef, is composed of a series of several quarry levels on which the more complex, anastomosing reef-core bodies can be dem- onstrated.

This locality is a small reef body about 8 m below the top of the Holston Formation. The Alcoa Highway reef core is about 3 m thick and 6 m wide; its associated cross-bedded flank deposits dip away from the core and extend to a feather edge 17 m to the north and 9 m to the south of the center of the core (pl. Surround- ing this small body are several much larger anastomosing core-Hank complexes; the upper core of one of these can be seen in the lower left corner of plate I-A. The small reef was chosen for treatment here be- cause a small number of samples serve to indicate the compositional variability within one reef mass. Plate I-B shows the core lithology

Figure 3 shows the location of analyzed samples and their compo- sition. The two facies differ markedly in their gross composition. Mud- stone matrix is virtually absent in the flank (pl. I-C), whereas it composes up to 45 volume percent of the core samples (pl. I-B). The distribution of interstitial sparry calcite cement (SG in fig, 3) is the inverse of muddy matrix. Bladed sparry calcite (RS in fig. 3) is considered here to repre- sent a product of diagenetic replacement, for it randomly cuts across the borders of other types of grains, In contrast, spar cement (SC in fig. 3) always has an obvious interstitial position and often shows the features of drusy-growth (Bathurst, 1958) indicating growth by void filling. Spar cement syntaxially overgrows pelmatozoan plates and colum- nals when these are abundant, giving the rock a crystalline texture, and producing the most impermeable type of commercial Holston Marble. The general taxonomic composition of the two facies differs (fig, 3). The ratio permatozoan debris-Bryozoa is about I/I in flank samples but ranges from I/2 to I/20 in core samples. Stromatactis structures (pl. I-D) are abundant in samples from the middle of the core but absent from flank deposits. As in stromatactis structures reported from other parts of the lower Paleozoic, these features are planar areas of sparry calcite with a flat base and an irregular top.
