The inclividual core masses of the Holston are of smaller scale than other Ordovician reefs previously described (for example, Pitcher, 1971; Rowell and Krause, 1972), but the Holston cores are not separate and distinct bodies like these other reefs. Instead, as they grew upward, the Holston cores formed an anastomosing network through the rock body (Fig. 6), eventually building a great mass of facies closely related to the core environment. Thus, the individual cores are analagous to large colonial coral-red algae masses in modern reefs. The adjacent Holston flank beds are analagous to the debris accumulations between more tightly bound masses in modern reefs. From this viewpoint, the individ- ual core masses are not reefs at all, but the greater mass composed of anastomosing cores ancl intervening clebris compose the actual reef, If this view is correct, then the Holston reef is a single genetic unit up to 100 m thick and at least 40 km long. COMPARISOlV WITH CHAZYAN REEFS

The Chazyan reefs of eastern New York state are of upper Lower Ordovician-Middle Ordovician age (upper Llanvirn-lower Llandeilo of the Welsh type Ordovician = upper White rock-Marmor-lower Ashby Stages of G. A. Cooper, 1956) according to Fisher (1962). Thus, these rocks are significantly older than the Holston Formation. However, the New York Chazy reefs are well described and of sufFiciently similar age to warrant a careful comparison with the Holston reefs. The reefal origin of parts of the Chazy was first recognized by Raymond (1924), and the stratigraphic relationships were first detailed by Oxley (1951) and Oxley and Kay (1959). The work of Pitcher (ms, 1964, 1971) is the most detailed analysis of the Chazyan environments and forms the basis for the following comparison.

The geometry and general environmental setting of the Chazyan reefs are markedly different from those in the Holston. The Chazyan reefs are uniformly rather small (2-4 x 2-10 m) except where the "reefal" mass forms a bedded deposit of wide extent. Rarely, the reef masses reach dimensions of 5 m thick, 8 m wide, and 50 m long. The reef masses are unbedded and composed of bryozoan-algal-stromatoporoid boundstone with occasional tabulate coral colonies sometimes forming a framework. The sediment that surrounds the boundstone masses is usually flat bedded, and the contact between boundstone and flanking beds is usually sharp. The unbedded boundstone core masses are sepa- rate and distinct, surrounded on all sides by bedded skeletal limestones, and do not anastomose laterally or vertically. Most of the reefs never stood more than 0.5 to 1 m above the adjacent sea bottom. Pitcher' s interpretation of tlie environment of these reefs places them in a shelf area and laterally equivalent to nearby cross-bedded quartz sands, oolite deposits, and intertidal mudflats. Tlius, as pointed out by Pitcher (ms, p. 4), these reefs represent analogues to modern shelf-lagoon patch reefs. The Silurian reefs of North America (I.owenstam, 1957) and Gotland (Manten, 1971), although much larger in size, also were located within a shelf environment. These contrast witli Devonian reefs which often had a shelf-edge location as in large modern reefs (compare Playford and Lowry, 1966). Pitcher (ms, p. 4) concluded that these data indicated a trend in reef evolution from on-shelf locations in the Ordovician and Silurian to shelf-edge locations in the Devonian. To judge from our study of the Holston and associatecl lithologies, reef evolution had progressed to the point of shelf-edge reefs at least by the time the Holston reefs formed.
