Although table 5 gives a reasonable representation of the average dominance in all core collections, the composition of the fauna changes upward in the quarry. We have, therefore, analyzed the collections at five levels within the quarry which were produced by the original quarrying operations but are approximately evenly spaced vertically. These levels can be seen in plate 2. Figure 5A shows the change in mean-rank-abundance of core genera upward in Deane Quarry as well as the growth forzn of each genus. Batostoma dominates the core fauna at most levels, while the seconcl most abundant genus is different at each level. The third dominance position is held by a different genus at almost every level. Some genera seem to have an inverse dominance relationship, for example when Mesotryfza is abundant, AmPlexopora is rare and vice versa. Other genera appear to show parallel variations in abundance as in the case of AmPlexoPora and IIemiphragma. Constellaria (pl. 3-I) maintains its position as the fifth to seventh most abundant genus throughout the quarry, MonotryPa decreases upward in abundance, while Bythopora increases upward. Nicholsonella, a genus that characteristically shows granular microstructure indicating replace- ment (see ihlcKinney, 1971) and probably original unstable {aragoniticP) mineral composition, is virtually absent at the lowest four levels. In the upper level collections, however, this genus quite suddenly becomes the dominant form. This marked shift in faunal composition corresponds with the transition into the overlying quartzose Chapman Ridge Forma- tion. Although this formation is much thinner in the quarry than else- where, it marks the sudden introduction of abundant terrigenous clastics into the area and probably increased sedimentation rate. Lagaaj and Gautier (1965) have shown the importance of sedimentation rate in controlling the distribution of modern bryozoans, so that the change in the Holston fauna probably reflects a change in this environmental factor.
