INTRODUCTION

The "Holston Marble" of East Tennessee (fig. I) has been quarried for lime and building stone since colonial times. This white to red, massive, coarse-grained limestone constitutes an important resource as well as a conspicuous unit within the Middle Ordovician section of the Southern Appalachians; yet little is known about the stratigraphy, biological composition, or origin of the formation.

The Holston Formation has long been the subject of controversy. The earlier interpretations are summarized below and in figure 2. Even the nomenclature of this unit has been in question; it has been variously known as the Holston, Farragut, or Red Knobs Formation and combina- tions of Holston-Tellico or Holston-Chapman Ridge formations. ABSTRACT. The precise age and environment of deposition of the middle Ordo- vician Holston Formation of east Tennessee have long been problematical. Earlier workers have suggested an age of upper Porterfieldian (lower Caradocian) based primarily on brachiopod biostratigraphy. Preliminary analysis of the bryozoans of the Holston (more than 80 genera have been recognized) suggests an age of upper Wilderness (middle Champlainian = middle Caradocian).

Although no detailed environmental studies were undertaken by earlier workers, some have suggested that the Holston is of reefal origin. The present study shows that the Holston formed as a major reef tract at the edge of a large carbonate bank and attained a length of more than 40 km. The reef mass is divided into deposits representing two subenvironments: (1) anastomosing bryozoan-boundstone masses for which we propose the term reef-core, and (2) intervening cross-bedded, pelmatozoan/bryozoan calcarenite for which we propose the term reef-flank. As with modern reefs, the flanking debris deposits compose about 75 to 80 percent of the entire mass and the boundstone cores about 20 to 25 percent. Stratigraphically the total reef mass composes a unit about 100 m thick. The reef cores consist of incrusting bryozoans (90 percent of all bryozoans in cores) as both binders and framework builders. The reef flanks are composed of fragments of ramose bryozoans (90 percent of all bryozoans in flanks) and pelmatozoan debris. Throughout the entire 100-m section, six bryozoan communities have been recognized in the reef cores and asso- ciated flanks based on the mean-rank-abundance of bryozoan genera. These commu- nities are arranged in vertical sequence and are listed in order of decreasing mean- rank-abundance of the contained genera. The lower communities are (A) reef-core community consisting of Batostoma MonotryPa MesotryPa Constellaria Stictipora DiPlotryPal (B) associated reef-flank community consisting of Batostoma StictiPora CalofIora EridotryPa. The middle communities are: (A) reef core consisting of Batostoma-BythoPora MonotryPa StictoPora AmPlexoPora HemiPhragma; (B) associ- ated reef flank consisting of Stictopora Bythopora Calopora Hemiphragma. The up- per communities are: (A) reef core consisting of Nicholsonellat MesotryPa Bythopora Batostoma Constellaria; (B) associated reef flank consisting of StictoPora CaloPora- Batostoma Eridotrypa. The differences in relative abundance of the bryozoan genera is believed to reflect changes in sedimentation rate.

Comparison of this reef with other Ordovician reefs indicates that the Holston masses are of larger scale, have a greater generic but lower phylum-level diversity, and are probably unique in having a shelf-edge location rather than an on-shelf location.
