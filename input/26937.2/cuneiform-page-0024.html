From the physical and biological evidence present in these rocks, a reasonable inference of the Holston reef environment can be made. A three-dimensional reconstruction of the reefs is por- trayed as a block diagram in Figure 6. At any one time, the environment was composed of domate areas populated by complex communities of incrusting bryozoan colonies. These grew upward more rapidly than sediment accumulated in intervening areas, occasionally developing ver- tical margins incrusted by Bryozoa. The activity of the Eryozoa not only produced the framework of bryozoan boundstone masses but acted as binders to stabilize mud and fossil debris which accumulated on the irregular surface of the boundstone domes. The boundstone masses had the ecological potential to have been wave resistant and thus flit the definition of reef given by Nelson, Brown, and Brineman (1962). Adja- cent to the raised boundstone masses and perhaps on them as well, lived a dense thicket of pelmatozoa. As these animals died and their external epidermis decayed, columnals and calyx plates were liberated to ac- cumulate in cross beds which sloped downward and away from the

Schopf (1969, p. 237) has summarized the iinportant ecological factors for bryozoans as tem- perature, salinity, turbulence, rate of sedimentation, factors involving light, dissolved gasses, and substrate type. Of these, turbulence or wave energy is the only factor that might reasonably have varied in the short distance separating the Ilolston core and flank environments. Stack (1936) noted that incrusting colonial forms tend to dominate in areas of turbulent water wliile ramose forms indicate quieter water.
