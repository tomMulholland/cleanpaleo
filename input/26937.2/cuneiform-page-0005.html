It has been widely referred to as argillaceous limestone but contains only a few percent of clay (J. Stephenson, 1972, personal commun.}. There is no physical evidence for a hiatus, and the upward changes in the lithology, most of which are gradual, are sufficient to explain the upward changes in benthic fauna, brachiopods included. Pending further environmental studies we consicler the entire unit to be a single unit, for which we prefer the name Lenoir Formation because of priority (Safford and Killebrew, 1876).

In the study area, the Lenoir varies regularly from base to top, and the unit can be broadly interpreted as a transgressive sequence. The basal Mosheim Member is 6 to 25 m (20-80 ft) thick and is composed of three lithologies (terins after Folk, 1959): (1) dismicrite, (2) algally laminated micrite, and (3) micrite without "birdseyes". All these lithologies usually have an artliropod-mollusc fauna, and probably rep- resent a supra-tidal to inter-tidal environment. The overlying lithology comprises the remainder of the 200-m (700-ft) formation and is difficult to subdivide because of. the gradational nature of lithologic contacts. In brief, the formation above the Mosheim varies from micrite at the base with abundant green and blue-green fossil algae to coarser grained inicritic calcisiltite or occasionally calcareniIe at the top (Walkei., 1971). TJIere is a general increase upward in percentage of worn and algally boretl pelmatozoan debris and transported ectoprocts. That these two faunal elements are not indigenous is suggested by their abracled condi- tion. The most logical source for these transported faunal elements is the environment in which the Holston Formation was deposited. At some localities (for example along Alcoa Highway near locality 4) the upper Lenoir and lower Holston are interbedded. Other aspects of the Lenoir (such as abundant green algae, thorough biogenic reworking, diverse sponge, and pleosponge fauna) suggest that the unit was de- posited in a shallow, lagoonal-type enviromnent. We, thus, believe that the Lenoir environment and the Holston environment must have beeI1 contemporaneous, at least in part. The Lenior was probably deposited in a shelf-lagoon shoreward of the Holston environment.
