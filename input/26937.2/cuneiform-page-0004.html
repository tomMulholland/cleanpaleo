B. N. Cooper (1944, p. 59-60; 1945) noted that what had been called Holston by earlier workers was a discontinuous lithofacies that occurred at different levels within the stratigraphic sequence. He, there- fore, suggested that the name be abandoned except as a lithologic {or facies) designation and that each separate unit of the lithology be given a distinct name. His work was apparently the first published recogni- tion of the disparate ages of Holston lithology in different fault-bounded strike belts and of the possible facies relationship between Holston lithology and other lithologies.

Prouty (1946), in a revision of the lower Middle Ordovician of southwest Virginia and northeast Tennessee, recognized the different ages of the Holston in the various strike-belts of east Tennessee and applied different names to these units. He proposed the term Farragut Formation for the quarried marble in the belt northwest of the Knox- ville Thrust Fault and gave a section along the Tennessee River near Farragut. as the type. He also applied this name southeast of the Fault {the area of the present study) in spite of a difference in lithology. His proposed nomenclature around Knoxville has not generally been accepted.

Rodgers (1955, p, 70 and 74) discussed the Holston "Marble" in the strike-belts northwest and southeast of the Knoxville Thrust. (Our research is restricted to the southeast.) He noted the improbability of the equivalency of the Tellico of Keith in these belts with the type Tellico Formation along the Tellico River in Monroe County, Tenn„ 56 km to the south-southwest. Rodgers thus mapped the "marble" and overlying quartzose calcarenite beds together as the Holston For- mation. While recognizing the different ages of the unit in various strike- belts, he felt that the gross-lithologic similarities from belt to belt warranted mapping the unit under a single name.
