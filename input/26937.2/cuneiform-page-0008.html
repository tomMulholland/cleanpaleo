HOLSTON ENVIRONMENT

General

The Holston as we define it in the study area contains two basic lithologies. The most striking is white to pink to red ectoproct (bryo- zoan) boundstone (term after Dunham, 1962) in which the rock is com- posed of abundant incrusting ectoprocts binding micrite and fossil debris into moundlike structures. When these mounds are separate from one another, they have vertical and horizontal dimensions measured in a few meters (pl. 1-A). They are, however, rarely entirely separate but usually anastomose, joining and splitting irregularly upward (pl. 2-A).
